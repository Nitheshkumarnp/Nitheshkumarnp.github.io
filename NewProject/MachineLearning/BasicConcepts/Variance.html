Variance¶

In Statistics¶

Variance is a measure of how much a set of values varies or deviates from their average (mean). It quantifies the spread or dispersion of a dataset.

Imagine you have two sets of exam scores for two different classes:

    Class A Scores: 85, 87, 88, 86, 87
    Class B Scores: 70, 90, 60, 95, 50
    Both classes might have the same average score, but their variance will differ:

Class A: Scores are close to each other, so the variance is low. The students’ scores are consistently near the average.

Class B: Scores are spread out widely from the average, so the variance is high. The students’ scores vary greatly from each other.

Why Variance is Useful:

    Understand Spread: It helps to understand how spread out the values in a dataset are. High variance means data points are spread out from the mean; low variance means they are close to the mean.

    Data Consistency: Variance helps assess the consistency of data. For example, in a production setting, low variance in product measurements means consistent quality.

    Risk Assessment: In finance, variance is used to measure the risk or volatility of investment returns. High variance indicates higher risk.

Summary:

    Variance measures how much the values in a dataset differ from the average value.
    High Variance: Values are spread out widely from the mean.
    Low Variance: Values are close to the mean.

In Machine Learning¶

Definition:

    In the context of machine learning models, variance refers to how much the model's predictions vary when trained on different subsets of the data.

Interpretation: High variance means the model is highly sensitive to the training data, leading to overfitting. Low variance means the model is stable and its predictions do not change much with different training datasets.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

# Generate sample data
np.random.seed(0)
X = np.random.rand(100, 1) * 10
y = 2 * X**2 + 3 * X + np.random.randn(100, 1) * 10

# Function to plot polynomial regression results
def plot_polynomial_regression(X, y, degree, ax):
    polynomial_features = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
    X_poly = polynomial_features.fit_transform(X)
    model = LinearRegression()
    model.fit(X_poly, y)
    y_poly_pred = model.predict(X_poly)
    
    ax.scatter(X, y, color='blue', label='Data points')
    ax.plot(X, y_poly_pred, color='red', label=f'Polynomial degree {degree}')
    ax.set_title(f"Polynomial Regression (Degree {degree})")
    ax.set_xlabel("X")
    ax.set_ylabel("y")
    ax.legend()

# Plotting results for different polynomial degrees
fig, axs = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(15, 5))

# Low variance model (Degree 2)
plot_polynomial_regression(X, y, degree=2, ax=axs[0])

# High variance model (Degree 10)
plot_polynomial_regression(X, y, degree=10, ax=axs[1])

plt.show()

Explanation

Low Variance Model (Degree 2):

    This model is simple and captures the general trend of the data.
    It may have higher bias but lower variance.
    The model doesn't overfit the data and provides a good fit for unseen data.

High Variance Model (Degree 10):

    This model is complex and fits the training data very closely, including the noise.
    It has low bias but high variance.
    The model overfits the data, which may lead to poor performance on new data.

Visualization

The plots will show the following:

Degree 2 Polynomial:

    A smooth curve that follows the general trend of the data points.
    Low variance and better generalization.

Degree 10 Polynomial:

    A wiggly curve that fits the data points closely, capturing noise.
    High variance and likely poor generalization to new data.
