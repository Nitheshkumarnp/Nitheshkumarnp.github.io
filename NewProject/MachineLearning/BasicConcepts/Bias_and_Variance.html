Bias and Variance Trade Off

The bias-variance trade-off is a fundamental concept in machine learning and statistical modeling that describes the balance between two sources of error that affect the performance of models: bias and variance.

Bias

    Definition: Bias refers to the error introduced by approximating a real-world problem, which may be complex, by a simplified model.
    Cause: High bias is often the result of a model that is too simple to capture the underlying patterns in the data.
    Effect: High bias can lead to underfitting, where the model performs poorly on both training and test data.

Variance

    Definition: Variance refers to the error introduced by the model's sensitivity to small fluctuations in the training dataset.
    Cause: High variance is often the result of a model that is too complex and captures the noise in the training data.
    Effect: High variance can lead to overfitting, where the model performs well on the training data but poorly on test data.

Trade-off

    Goal: The goal is to find the right balance between bias and variance to minimize the total error.
    Model Complexity: Simple models have high bias and low variance, while complex models have low bias and high variance.

Bias

Definition:

    Bias is the error introduced by assuming a simplified model for a complex problem.
    High bias occurs when the model makes strong assumptions about the data and simplifies it too much.

Causes:

    Using overly simple models that cannot capture the underlying patterns in the data.
    Examples include linear regression for non-linear relationships or decision stumps (one-level decision trees) for complex patterns.

Effects:

    High bias leads to underfitting, where the model performs poorly on both the training and test datasets.
    The model fails to capture the important trends in the data.

from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error

# Function to plot model predictions
def plot_model_predictions(degrees):
    plt.figure(figsize=(12, 10))
    
    for i, degree in enumerate(degrees, 1):
        polynomial_features = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
        X_poly = polynomial_features.fit_transform(X.reshape(-1, 1))
        
        model = LinearRegression()
        model.fit(X_poly, y)
        y_poly_pred = model.predict(X_poly)
        
        plt.subplot(3, 2, i)
        plt.plot(X, true_function(X), label="True function")
        plt.scatter(X, y, edgecolor='b', s=50, label="Data points")
        plt.plot(X, y_poly_pred, label=f"Model (degree={degree})")
        plt.xlabel("X")
        plt.ylabel("y")
        plt.title(f"Degree {degree}")
        plt.legend()
    
    plt.tight_layout()
    plt.show()

# Degrees of polynomial to plot
degrees = [1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11]
plot_model_predictions(degrees)

Polynomial degree 1: High bias (underfitting) the model is too simple to capture the pattern.

Polynomial degree 3: Good fit the model captures the pattern well.

Polynomial degree 5: Slight overfitting the model starts to capture noise.

Polynomial degree 7 and higher: High variance (overfitting) the model captures too much noise.

from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score

# Function to evaluate model performance using cross-validation
def evaluate_model_performance(degrees):
    train_errors = []
    test_errors = []
    
    for degree in degrees:
        polynomial_features = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
        X_poly = polynomial_features.fit_transform(X.reshape(-1, 1))
        
        model = LinearRegression()
        model.fit(X_poly, y)
        
        # Cross-validation scores
        train_score = -cross_val_score(model, X_poly, y, cv=5, scoring='neg_mean_squared_error').mean()
        train_errors.append(train_score)
        
        # Test error
        X_test = np.sort(np.random.rand(n_samples))
        y_test = true_function(X_test) + np.random.randn(n_samples) * 0.1
        X_test_poly = polynomial_features.transform(X_test.reshape(-1, 1))
        y_test_pred = model.predict(X_test_poly)
        test_score = mean_squared_error(y_test, y_test_pred)
        test_errors.append(test_score)
    
    # Plot training and test errors
    plt.figure(figsize=(6, 4))
    plt.plot(degrees, train_errors, label="Training error")
    plt.plot(degrees, test_errors, label="Test error")
    plt.xlabel("Model complexity (polynomial degree)")
    plt.ylabel("Mean Squared Error")
    plt.title("Bias-Variance Trade-off")
    plt.legend()
    plt.show()

# Degrees of polynomial to evaluate
degrees = list(range(1, 12))
evaluate_model_performance(degrees)

Understanding High Variance and Low Variance

Low Variance (High Bias) at Degree 1:

    Model: Linear regression (degree 1 polynomial).
    Behavior: The model is very simple and assumes a linear relationship. It doesn't capture the underlying complexity of the data.
    Effect: Predictions are consistent across different datasets but fail to capture the true relationship (underfitting).
    Calculation: Predictions don't change much if the training data changes slightly because the model is too simple.

Low Variance (Moderate Bias) at Degree 3:

    Model: Polynomial regression (degree 3).
    Behavior: The model captures the underlying pattern reasonably well without being too sensitive to noise.
    Effect: Predictions are more accurate and do not vary too much across different datasets.
    Calculation: Predictions are relatively stable, balancing complexity and generalization.

High Variance (Low Bias) at Degree 7:

    Model: Polynomial regression (degree 7).
    Behavior: The model is very complex and captures even the minor fluctuations in the training data (noise).
    Effect: Predictions vary significantly if the training data changes slightly (overfitting).
    Calculation: Predictions are highly sensitive to the training data, leading to large changes in the model with different datasets.

Understanding High Bias and Low Bias

High Bias at Degree 1:

    The linear regression does not capture the curve of the true function well.
    The model is too simple, resulting in a systematic error (high bias).
    It performs poorly on both training and test data, showing underfitting.

Low Bias (Polynomial Regression):

    The polynomial regression captures the curve of the true function well.
    The model is complex enough to fit the underlying pattern, reducing systematic error (low bias).
    It performs better on the training data, showing a good fit.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.preprocessing import PolynomialFeatures
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

# Set random seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)

# Generate synthetic data
def true_function(X):
    return np.sin(1.5 * np.pi * X)

# Data points
n_samples = 100
X = np.sort(np.random.rand(n_samples))
y = true_function(X) + np.random.randn(n_samples) * 0.1

# Function to fit and plot models on different subsets
def plot_model_variance(degree, n_subsets=5):
    plt.figure(figsize=(6, 4))
    
    for _ in range(n_subsets):
        # Generate a new subset of data
        X_subset = np.sort(np.random.rand(n_samples))
        y_subset = true_function(X_subset) + np.random.randn(n_samples) * 0.1
        
        # Fit the model
        polynomial_features = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
        X_poly = polynomial_features.fit_transform(X_subset.reshape(-1, 1))
        
        model = LinearRegression()
        model.fit(X_poly, y_subset)
        
        # Predict on the original X
        X_poly_orig = polynomial_features.transform(X.reshape(-1, 1))
        y_poly_pred = model.predict(X_poly_orig)
        
        # Plot the predictions
        plt.plot(X, y_poly_pred, label=f"Subset prediction", alpha=0.5)
    
    # Plot true function and data points
    plt.plot(X, true_function(X), label="True function", color='black')
    plt.scatter(X, y, edgecolor='b', s=50, label="Data points")
    plt.xlabel("X")
    plt.ylabel("y")
    plt.title(f"Model Predictions with Degree {degree}")
    plt.legend()
    plt.show()

# Plot variance for models of degree 1, 3, and 7
plot_model_variance(degree=1)
plot_model_variance(degree=3)
plot_model_variance(degree=7)

# Function to calculate variance of model predictions
def calculate_variance(degree, n_subsets=100):
    predictions = []
    
    for _ in range(n_subsets):
        # Generate a new subset of data
        X_subset = np.sort(np.random.rand(n_samples))
        y_subset = true_function(X_subset) + np.random.randn(n_samples) * 0.1
        
        # Fit the model
        polynomial_features = PolynomialFeatures(degree=degree)
        X_poly = polynomial_features.fit_transform(X_subset.reshape(-1, 1))
        
        model = LinearRegression()
        model.fit(X_poly, y_subset)
        
        # Predict on the original X
        X_poly_orig = polynomial_features.transform(X.reshape(-1, 1))
        y_poly_pred = model.predict(X_poly_orig)
        
        predictions.append(y_poly_pred)
    
    # Calculate variance of predictions
    predictions = np.array(predictions)
    variance = np.var(predictions, axis=0)
    return np.mean(variance)

# Calculate variance for models of degree 1, 3, and 7
variance_degree_1 = calculate_variance(degree=1)
variance_degree_3 = calculate_variance(degree=3)
variance_degree_7 = calculate_variance(degree=7)

Summary

    Bias: Systematic error introduced by overly simplistic models.

    Variance in Statistics: Measures the spread of data points around the mean.
    Variance in Machine Learning: Measures how much a models predictions change when trained on different datasets.

    High Bias: Results from models that are too simple, leading to underfitting and poor performance on both training and test data.
    Low Bias: Achieved by using more complex models that can capture the underlying patterns in the data.

    Low Variance (High Bias): Simple models (e.g., linear regression) have consistent predictions across different datasets but may underfit the true relationship.
    Moderate Variance (Optimal Bias-Variance Trade-off): Moderately complex models (e.g., degree 3 polynomial) balance capturing the true relationship and generalizing well to new data.
    High Variance (Low Bias): Complex models (e.g., degree 7 polynomial) are highly sensitive to the training data, capturing noise and leading to overfitting.
